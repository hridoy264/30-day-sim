## Build Your Portfolio

Skills you can't show don't count. Turn your 30 days into proof:

1. **Put it on GitHub.** Push your day-by-day code and your capstone. A green commit history tells a story.
2. **Write up the capstone.** A clear README with a GIF of it working is worth more than a paragraph of claims.

## Where to Go Next

You have a strong foundation. Pick directions based on what excited you:

### If you loved manipulation (PyBullet/MuJoCo)
- Deeper RL: PPO/SAC tuning, imitation learning, [Gymnasium-Robotics](https://robotics.farama.org/) environments.
- Real grasping research, dexterous hands, and motion planning.

### If you loved the robotics stack (Gazebo/ROS 2)
- **Nav2** — the full ROS 2 navigation stack (mapping, localization, path planning).
- **MoveIt 2** — motion planning for arms.
- **SLAM** — building maps from sensor data.

### If you loved learning (RL)
- The Sutton & Barto textbook (free) for the theory.
- Large-scale RL with **Isaac Lab** on a GPU.
- Sim-to-real projects on a real low-cost robot.

### Go bigger
- Get a cheap real robot (a small rover, an arm kit) and apply sim-to-real for real.
- Contribute to an open-source simulator or robot model.
- Build a digital twin of something in your life.

## Habits of a Great Simulation Engineer

Keep these as you continue:

- **Start simple, add one thing at a time.** The lesson of every mini-project.
- **Test continuously.** Small bugs are easy; compounded bugs are nightmares.
- **Watch your time step and your units.** Half of all sim bugs live here.
- **Visualize everything.** If you can see it (RViz, plots, the GUI), you can debug it.
- **Keep building.** Skill compounds. One small project a month keeps you sharp.
